July 2026

Design Considerations For The Electrical Subsystem of a Stardome Projector.

When people step into a planetarium, they are amazed by the beautiful night sky projected above them. Behind this experience is a combination of engineering, science, and technology working together. A Stardome projector is much more than a source of light—it’s a system that brings astronomy closer to people through careful design and innovation. As part of the AstroCET Stardome Project, our team is currently working on the electrical subsystem. Since the project is still in its research and development phase, I started by understanding the complete working of a Stardome projector before moving on to the electrical design. The first stage of my research was learning how a Stardome projector creates a realistic projection of stars and constellations. I studied the different subsystems involved, including the electrical, optical, and mechanical sections, to understand how they work together to produce a high-quality projection.

My primary focus was the electrical subsystem, especially the light source. I researched different lighting technologies such as standard LEDs, high-power LEDs, and Chip-on-Board (COB) LEDs. While comparing these options, I looked at important parameters including brightness, luminous efficiency, colour temperature, power consumption, lifespan, beam angle, and heat generation. This helped me understand that selecting the right light source is a balance between performance, efficiency, reliability, and cost. Thermal management became another important area of study. Since high-power LEDs generate considerable heat, I researched different cooling methods, including aluminium heat sinks, thermal interface materials, and airflow techniques. Understanding these concepts highlighted how proper thermal management directly affects both the performance and longevity of electronic components.

Although my main responsibility is the electrical subsystem, I also explored the optical aspects of the projector. I studied projection lenses, beam shaping, optical alignment, and light distribution to understand how these factors influence image quality. Looking at technical figures, diagrams, and existing projector designs helped me connect theoretical concepts with real engineering applications. As my research progressed, I also explored the architecture of commercial planetarium systems, component layouts, and engineering design approaches. These references provided valuable insights into how professional systems are developed and how different engineering disciplines come together in a single product. One of the biggest lessons I have learned is that engineering starts long before implementation. Careful research, analysing technical resources, comparing available technologies, and understanding design trade-offs are all essential steps before building a system. Every paper, datasheet, figure, and technical document I studied added another piece to the overall picture. The Stardome project is still under development, and there is much more to explore as we move towards the design and implementation stages. This research has strengthened my understanding of electrical engineering while also introducing me to the fascinating relationship between electronics, optics, and astronomy. This blog marks the beginning of my research journey on the Stardome projector. As the project progresses, I look forward to sharing more about our design decisions, challenges, and the development of the electrical subsystem.
